# Connectivity & I/O

> **Part of:** [Motherboard](./index) · [Hardware Fundamentals](../index)

A modern motherboard is a hub for a surprising number of I/O standards. Understanding what each port does — and crucially, where the bandwidth comes from — determines how to build and troubleshoot systems.

---

## USB Standards

USB version names are notoriously confusing. The USB-IF renamed everything in 2019, but vendors still use both old and new names interchangeably.

| Marketing name | USB-IF name | Speed | Common use |
|---------------|------------|-------|-----------|
| USB 2.0 | USB 2.0 | 480 Mb/s (~60 MB/s) | Keyboards, mice, most dongles |
| USB 3.0 / USB 3.1 Gen 1 / USB 3.2 Gen 1 | USB 3.2 Gen 1 | 5 Gb/s (~500 MB/s) | Flash drives, older SSDs |
| USB 3.1 Gen 2 / USB 3.2 Gen 2 | USB 3.2 Gen 2 | 10 Gb/s (~1,000 MB/s) | Fast external SSDs |
| USB 3.2 Gen 2×2 | USB 3.2 Gen 2×2 | 20 Gb/s | High-speed external storage |
| USB 4 Gen 2×2 | USB4 20 | 20 Gb/s | Thunderbolt 3 compatible |
| USB 4 Gen 3×2 | USB4 40 | 40 Gb/s | Thunderbolt 4 compatible |
| USB 4 Gen 4 | USB4 80 | 80 Gb/s | Thunderbolt 5 compatible |

**Connector types:**
- **USB-A** — the classic rectangular plug (2.0 through 3.2 Gen 2)
- **USB-C** — the oval reversible connector (can carry USB 2.0 all the way up to USB4 Gen 4, or Thunderbolt — the connector alone tells you nothing about speed)
- **USB-B** — square plug on printers/hubs (older devices)
- **Micro-USB / Mini-USB** — legacy mobile devices

**Key rule:** A USB-C port on the back of your PC could be USB 2.0 speeds (480 Mb/s) or 40 Gb/s Thunderbolt. Always check the spec sheet or look for the Thunderbolt lightning bolt icon.

## Thunderbolt

Thunderbolt is Intel's high-bandwidth peripheral standard, now co-developed with USB4. It runs over USB-C connectors but is a distinct protocol with higher guarantees.

| Version | Speed | PCIe lanes | Display support | Compatible with |
|---------|-------|-----------|----------------|----------------|
| **TB3** | 40 Gb/s | PCIe 3.0 ×4 | Up to 2× 4K | USB-C |
| **TB4** | 40 Gb/s | PCIe 3.0 ×4 | Up to 2× 4K (or 1× 8K) | USB4 Gen 2×2, USB-C |
| **TB5** | 120 Gb/s (80 Gb/s base) | PCIe 4.0 ×4 | Up to 3× 4K 144Hz | USB4 Gen 4, USB-C |

**What Thunderbolt enables that USB alone cannot:**
- **PCIe tunnelling:** External GPU enclosures, PCIe NVMe docks, professional audio interfaces
- **Daisy chaining:** Up to 6 devices on one TB4 cable
- **Certified cables:** TB4 cables must pass strict timing/signal quality tests — cheap cables won't work

**macOS note:** All Apple Silicon Macs include Thunderbolt 3/4/5 natively. On Windows/Linux, Thunderbolt support depends on the motherboard having a dedicated Intel JHL controller or an Intel CPU with integrated TB support.

## Ethernet / Wired Networking

| Standard | Speed | Notes |
|---------|-------|-------|
| **Gigabit Ethernet (1GbE)** | 1,000 Mb/s (~125 MB/s) | Obsolete for new builds — still common on budget boards |
| **2.5 Gigabit Ethernet (2.5GbE)** | 2,500 Mb/s (~310 MB/s) | Consumer standard since ~2021. Mid-range boards and above. |
| **5 Gigabit Ethernet (5GbE)** | 5,000 Mb/s | Rare on motherboards — niche step between 2.5G and 10G |
| **10 Gigabit Ethernet (10GbE)** | 10,000 Mb/s (~1,250 MB/s) | High-end boards — pairs well with 10G NAS |

**Real-world numbers:** Wired 2.5GbE will saturate a typical Wi-Fi 6/6E connection and handles NAS workloads easily. 10GbE is useful for video editors pulling large files off a shared NAS or network storage at near-SSD speeds.

**Common onboard controllers:**
- Intel I225-V / I226-V — 2.5GbE (common on Intel and AMD boards)
- Intel I219-V — 1GbE
- Realtek RTL8125 — 2.5GbE (budget alternative)
- Marvell AQC107 / AQC113 — 10GbE (high-end boards)

## Wi-Fi

Wi-Fi modules on modern motherboards are typically M.2-form-factor cards using the CNVi (Intel) or PCIe interface internally:

| Standard | Protocol | Max theoretical throughput | Key features |
|---------|---------|--------------------------|-------------|
| **Wi-Fi 5** | 802.11ac | 3.5 Gbps | 5 GHz only, MU-MIMO |
| **Wi-Fi 6** | 802.11ax | 9.6 Gbps | 2.4 & 5 GHz, OFDMA, 1024-QAM |
| **Wi-Fi 6E** | 802.11ax | 9.6 Gbps | + 6 GHz band (less interference) |
| **Wi-Fi 7** | 802.11be | 46 Gbps | Multi-link operation, 320 MHz channels |

**Practical rule:** Real-world Wi-Fi 6E throughput is ~1.5–2.5 Gbps at close range, typically 300–900 Mbps at typical home distances. For desktop builds within cable reach, wired 2.5GbE always beats wireless — lower latency, no interference, no congestion.

**Antennas:** Wi-Fi modules require external antenna connectors — the two small SMA connectors on the rear I/O that you attach the bundled antenna to. Skipping the antenna drops signal quality significantly.

## Rear I/O Panel — Port Identification Guide

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ [PS/2] [USB2][USB2]  [USB-A 3.2] [USB-A 3.2] [USB-C TB4]│
│                                                           │
│ [HDMI]  [DP] [DP]     [2.5GbE RJ-45]   [WIFI ANTENNAS]  │
│                                                           │
│        [LINE OUT] [LINE IN] [MIC]  [SPDIF OPT]           │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Colour coding (legacy, not always followed):**
- Blue = USB 3.0 (5 Gbps)
- Black = USB 2.0
- Teal / light blue = USB 3.2 Gen 2 (10 Gbps)
- Green port = Line Out (speakers/headphones)
- Pink port = Microphone In
- Blue port = Line In (recording from external source)

## Internal Headers

These are the connectors on the motherboard surface that you plug case cables into:

| Header | Function | Location on board |
|--------|---------|------------------|
| **Front Panel (F_PANEL)** | Power button, reset button, power LED, HDD activity LED | Bottom-right area |
| **USB 2.0 Header** | Front-panel USB-A 2.0 ports | Bottom edge |
| **USB 3.2 Gen 1 Header (19-pin)** | Front-panel USB-A 5 Gbps | Mid-right edge |
| **USB-C Front Panel Header** | Front-panel USB-C | Mid-edge (Type-E connector) |
| **ARGB Header (3-pin, 5V)** | Addressable RGB lighting | Multiple locations |
| **RGB Header (4-pin, 12V)** | Non-addressable RGB | Multiple locations |
| **TPM Header** | Discrete Trusted Platform Module | Near chipset |
| **Thunderbolt Header** | Add-in TB expansion card | Top area |
| **SATA power / reset** | Some boards expose reset via JBAT header | Near battery |

**Front panel wiring:** The front panel connector is almost always the most frustrating part of a new build — tiny individual 1-pin or 2-pin connectors that must be placed exactly right for power/reset buttons and LEDs. Refer to your motherboard manual's front panel diagram (it's always in there).

## Onboard Audio

Integrated audio codecs do a good job for most users. The analog circuitry is physically separated from the digital section of the board by a ground plane isolation cut (visible as a gap in the PCB layers) to reduce noise pickup.

| Codec | Board tier | SNR | Notes |
|-------|-----------|-----|-------|
| Realtek ALC897 | Budget | ~90 dB | Acceptable for headsets |
| Realtek ALC1220 | Mid-range | ~120 dB | Good for most headphones up to ~150Ω |
| ESS ES9218 / SABRE DAC | High-end | 120–130 dB | Asus SupremeFX, Gigabyte AMP-UP |

**When onboard audio isn't enough:**
- High-impedance headphones (>150Ω, e.g. Sennheiser HD600) benefit from a dedicated headphone amplifier
- Recording/podcasting benefits from a USB audio interface (dedicated ADC, lower noise floor)
- Audiophile setups use an external DAC/amp stack or a PCIe sound card (e.g. ASUS Xonar)
